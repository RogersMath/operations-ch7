Foundation Core Concepts

Process Flow Vocabulary

A process flow is a network of activities that a customer or unit of work moves through from start to finish. Each activity has a processing time — how long it takes to serve one customer. The bottleneck is the single activity with the lowest capacity, meaning the highest processing time per server. It limits the throughput of the entire process regardless of how fast every other step runs.

Understanding this is the difference between optimizing the right thing and wasting effort speeding up steps that are not the constraint.

Activity Capacity
Cap = (60 min/hr) ÷ Processing Time
Parallel Servers
Cap = Servers × (60 ÷ Time per server)
Process Throughput
Throughput = Capacity of Bottleneck
Process Flow Time
Flow Time = Sum of all activity times on the path
◆

Bottleneck ≠ slowest step — it's the lowest-capacity step. Capacity accounts for how many servers are at a step. Two slow servers can outrun one fast server. Always convert processing time to capacity (customers/hour) before comparing steps.

Flow Time

Total time one customer spends in the process from first step to last — also called throughput time or cycle time (per customer). For a sequential process, it's the sum of all activity times on the customer's path.

Throughput Rate

How many customers the process can serve per unit of time — limited entirely by the bottleneck activity's capacity. All other steps could run at full capacity; if the bottleneck is constrained, the whole process is constrained.

Bottleneck

The activity with the lowest capacity (customers/hour). In a process with parallel servers at one step, that step's capacity is the sum of all parallel server capacities. The bottleneck is where demand exceeds, or comes closest to exceeding, capacity.

Pooled vs. Dedicated

Parallel servers can be pooled (any customer can go to any server — more efficient) or dedicated (each server handles a specific customer type). Pooling always equals or improves throughput because idle capacity at one server can be used by demand for another.

Process Type Sequential Processes

Every Customer Follows the Same Path

In a sequential process, every customer goes through every step in order. There are no branches. The flow time is simply the sum of all processing times. The bottleneck is the step with the highest processing time — which gives it the lowest capacity (customers per hour).

Worked Example — Sequential Lakeview Car Wash

A car wash has four sequential steps: Pre-rinse (3 min) → Soap & scrub (7 min) → Final rinse (4 min) → Air dry (5 min). Each step has exactly one machine.

  1. Flow time (time one car spends in the process): 
    Flow Time = 3 + 7 + 4 + 5 = 19 minutes
  2. Find capacity of each step (cars per hour = 60 ÷ processing time): 
    Pre-rinse:   60 ÷ 3  = 20.0 cars/hr
Soap & scrub: 60 ÷ 7  = 8.6 cars/hr  ← lowest = bottleneck
Final rinse: 60 ÷ 4  = 15.0 cars/hr
Air dry:     60 ÷ 5  = 12.0 cars/hr
  3. Process throughput = bottleneck capacity: 
    Throughput = 8.6 cars/hour  (limited by Soap & scrub)
◆

Speeding up Pre-rinse from 3 min to 1 min would have zero effect on throughput. Only improvements to the bottleneck — Soap & scrub — can increase how many cars the process handles per hour.

Process Type Parallel Resources at a Step

Multiple Servers at the Same Step

When a step has multiple servers working in parallel, each serving different customers simultaneously, the capacity of that step multiplies. A step with 3 servers each taking 15 minutes has capacity 3 × (60 ÷ 15) = 12 customers/hour — not 4.

The bottleneck is still the step with the lowest total capacity. Parallel servers are one of the primary tools for relieving a bottleneck without changing individual processing times.

Step with N parallel servers
Capacity = N × (60 ÷ Time per server)
To find N needed
N ≥ Required Rate × (Time ÷ 60)
Worked Example — Parallel Servers Northgate Urgent Care

Patients flow through: Check-in (4 min, 1 clerk) → Nurse triage (10 min, 2 nurses) → Doctor exam (20 min, 3 doctors) → Checkout (3 min, 1 clerk).

  1. Convert each step to capacity (customers/hour), accounting for parallel servers: 
    Check-in:  1 × (60 ÷  4) = 1 × 15.0 = 15.0 patients/hr
Triage:    2 × (60 ÷ 10) = 2 ×  6.0 = 12.0 patients/hr
Doctor:    3 × (60 ÷ 20) = 3 ×  3.0 =  9.0 patients/hr  ← bottleneck
Checkout:  1 × (60 ÷  3) = 1 × 20.0 = 20.0 patients/hr
  2. Flow time (one path through the process): 
    Flow Time = 4 + 10 + 20 + 3 = 37 minutes
  3. Throughput = bottleneck capacity: 
    Throughput = 9 patients/hour  (limited by Doctor step)
⚠

Common error: Students often compute 3 doctors × 20 min = 60 min and conclude throughput is 1 patient/hour. That's the capacity of one doctor, not the step. Always multiply: 3 × (60 ÷ 20) = 9 patients/hour.

Process Type Diverging Paths & Customer Types

Different Customers, Different Routes

Many real processes split after a shared entry point. Some steps are shared by all customers; others are used only by specific customer types. When paths diverge, you must track each customer type's flow time separately (their unique path, start to finish) but identify the bottleneck across the whole process.

For shared steps with parallel dedicated servers (one server per customer type), the capacity calculation treats each server independently for its assigned customer stream — unless the servers are pooled.

Flow Time (by type)
Sum times on that customer's specific path only
Shared step capacity
Cap = Total servers at step × (60 ÷ Time)
Bottleneck (whole process)
Lowest capacity step across all paths
Worked Example — Diverging Paths Horizon Tax Filing Service

All clients start at Intake (5 min, 1 clerk). Then they split: Simple returns go to an Associate (12 min); Complex returns go to a Senior Advisor (25 min). Both paths end at Document Sign-off (4 min, 1 clerk). There is 1 Associate and 1 Senior Advisor. Assume equal mix of Simple and Complex.

  1. Flow time — Simple return path: 
    Intake + Associate + Sign-off = 5 + 12 + 4 = 21 minutes
  2. Flow time — Complex return path: 
    Intake + Sr. Advisor + Sign-off = 5 + 25 + 4 = 34 minutes
  3. Capacity at each step (equal 50/50 customer mix — shared steps serve both types): 
    Intake:      1 × (60 ÷  5) = 12.0 clients/hr  (all clients)
Associate:   1 × (60 ÷ 12) =  5.0 simple/hr
Sr. Advisor: 1 × (60 ÷ 25) =  2.4 complex/hr  ← bottleneck
Sign-off:    1 × (60 ÷  4) = 15.0 clients/hr  (all clients)
  4. Process throughput is constrained by the Senior Advisor: 
    Complex throughput: 2.4/hr
Matching simple:    2.4/hr  (equal mix)
Total throughput:   4.8 clients/hr
◆

When paths diverge, the bottleneck constrains its entire stream. Here the Senior Advisor limits complex returns to 2.4/hr. With an equal-mix constraint, this caps total throughput at 4.8/hr even though Intake could handle 12/hr and Sign-off could handle 15/hr.

Textbook Problems Worked Examples

Problem Type: Parallel Servers at One Step Bill's Barbershop (Fig. 7.9)

All customers pass through B1 (10 min) then B2 (8 min). Then they are served by either B3-a (15 min) or B3-b (10 min) — two parallel barbers. Finally all go through B4 (9 min).

  1. Flow time — there are two possible paths. The average customer time uses the average time at B3: 
    Avg B3 time = (15 + 10) ÷ 2 = 12.5 min
Flow Time   = B1 + B2 + avg(B3) + B4
            = 10 + 8 + 12.5 + 9 = 39.5 minutes
  2. Capacity at each step: 
    B1:  1 server × (60 ÷ 10)  =  6.0 customers/hr
B2:  1 server × (60 ÷  8)  =  7.5 customers/hr
B3:  2 servers — but different speeds, calculate total:
     (60÷15) + (60÷10) = 4.0 + 6.0 = 10.0 customers/hr
B4:  1 server × (60 ÷  9)  =  6.7 customers/hr  ← bottleneck
  3. Bottleneck is B4 at 6.7 customers/hr — not B3 (which is the most complex step visually), not B1 (slowest individual server). 
    Process throughput = 6.7 customers/hour  (~6–7 customers/hr)
◆

B3 has two servers with different speeds. Don't average them or treat them as one. Add their individual capacities: 4.0 + 6.0 = 10.0 customers/hr. This is much higher than B4's 6.7/hr, which is why B4 is the actual bottleneck despite being a single step with a reasonable processing time.

Problem Type: Diverging Paths + Parallel Cashiers Melissa's Photo Studio (Fig. 7.10)

All customers: Registration (5 min). Then pay at either Cashier A (5 min) or Cashier B (7 min) — pooled, any customer may use either. Then split: Individual portrait (15 min) or Group portrait (20 min). All end at Pick up portrait (7 min). Equal mix of individual and group.

  1. Flow time — Group portrait path: 
    Avg cashier time = (5 + 7) ÷ 2 = 6 min  (pooled, average)
Group flow time  = 5 + 6 + 20 + 7 = 38 minutes
  2. Capacity at each step (equal individual/group mix): 
    Registration: 1 × (60÷5)  = 12.0/hr
Cashiers:  (60÷5)+(60÷7) = 12.0 + 8.6 = 20.6/hr  (pooled total)
Individual:   1 × (60÷15) =  4.0 individuals/hr
Group:        1 × (60÷20) =  3.0 groups/hr  ← bottleneck
Pick up:      1 × (60÷7)  =  8.6/hr
  3. Bottleneck is the Group portrait step at 3.0/hr. 
    Group capacity:  3.0/hr
Matching indiv:  3.0/hr  (equal mix)
Total throughput: 6.0 customers/hr
  4. Bottleneck capacity for each type: 
    Individual stream bottleneck: 4.0/hr  (Individual portrait step)
Group stream bottleneck:      3.0/hr  (Group portrait step)
Overall process bottleneck:   Group portrait, 3.0/hr
◆

Pooled cashiers vs. dedicated portrait rooms. The cashiers are pooled — both serve all customers — so their combined capacity is 20.6/hr, which is not the bottleneck. The portrait rooms are dedicated (one per type), so each is constrained by its own processing time independently. Group portrait is the binding constraint for the whole system.
